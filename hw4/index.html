Overview

In this homework, we implemented a cloth simulation using a mass-spring system. In Part 1, we constructed a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs. In Part 2, we simulated motion using Verlet integration and added damping and spring constraints. In Part 3, we handled collisions with external objects (spheres and planes) by projecting point masses to valid positions outside the primitives. In Part 4, we implemented self-collision using spatial hashing to efficiently resolve intersections within the cloth. Finally, in Part 5, we created real-time shaders in GLSL to visualize the cloth with lighting and surface detail, including diffuse, Blinn-Phong, bump, displacement, and environment-mapped shading. We faced challenges with collision resolution and normal visualization in shaders but solved them through debugging vector math and checking coordinate space consistency.

To handle collisions with spheres, we first checked whether a point mass was inside the sphere by comparing its distance from the sphere’s center to the sphere’s radius. If it was inside, we calculated the tangent point on the sphere’s surface in the direction from the center to the point mass’s last position. We then computed a correction vector that would push the point just outside the sphere, scaled down by the friction factor. This allowed the cloth to make smooth, friction-aware contact with the sphere.

For planes, we determined a collision occurred if a point mass moved from one side of the plane to the other between frames. In that case, we computed the tangent point on the plane and corrected the point mass’s position to lie slightly above it using a small offset constant. Friction was again applied to scale the correction vector. This kept the cloth above the plane and prevented jittering or sinking into the surface.

With the default spring constant KS = 5000, the cloth draped naturally over the sphere. Lowering KS to 500 made the cloth much looser and caused it to conform more closely to the sphere’s shape. Raising KS to 50000 made the cloth significantly stiffer — it touched the sphere but retained a flatter, less flexible form.

Self-collisions: to avoid the inefficiency of an O(n^2) brute-force comparison between all point masses, we implemented spatial hashing. We divided the 3D space into a grid of boxes and assigned each point mass to a box using a hash function. The key for each box was computed using the point’s position, scaled and truncated to box-sized intervals. This allowed us to limit collision checks to point masses within the same spatial region.

We first completed the hash_position method to assign each 3D coordinate to a unique float key. Then, in build_spatial_map, we iterated over all point masses and inserted them into a hash map according to their spatial bin.

In self_collide, we checked for point masses that were closer than 2 * thickness within the same bin. If any were found, we computed a correction vector to move the current point mass away from overlapping neighbors. We averaged all such corrections and scaled them by 1 / simulation_steps to make the adjustment smoother over time.

Finally, we updated Cloth::simulate to call self_collide on each point mass in every simulation step.

With self-collision enabled, we observed that the cloth in selfCollision.json no longer passed through itself and instead folded and stacked more realistically. In early frames, the cloth formed visible folds when it contacted itself. Over time, it continued to shift slightly due to the lack of perfect energy loss, but the overall shape remained consistent and no longer exhibited clipping. We also tested how self-collision behaved under different simulation parameters: Low density made the cloth lighter and more likely to fold and flutter, leading to more frequent self-collisions. High density caused the cloth to drop heavily and press against itself more firmly, creating flatter folds with stronger overlap. Low ks made the cloth more flexible, so it folded more easily and exhibited softer contact. High ks made the cloth stiffer, which resulted in fewer but sharper and more defined folds.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program runs on the GPU and controls how objects are drawn on the screen. It has two parts: the vertex shader and the fragment shader. The vertex shader processes each vertex of a 3D object. It moves the vertex into screen space and passes information like position, normal, and texture coordinates to the next stage. The fragment shader runs for each pixel on the object’s surface. It uses the data from the vertex shader to calculate the final color of that pixel, including effects like lighting and textures. Together, they let us create smooth shading, reflections, textures, and other visual effects in real time.

In the diffuse shader, we calculated the lighting using Lambert’s cosine law. We computed the light direction and took the dot product with the surface normal to get the intensity. The result produced soft shading based on how directly a surface faces the light source. We extended the diffuse shader to support Blinn-Phong shading. The Blinn-Phong shading model is used to simulate how light interacts with a surface. It includes three parts: Ambient: a constant color that represents indirect light, so the object is not completely dark. Diffuse: depends on the angle between the surface and the light, creating soft shading where the light hits directly. Specular: produces shiny highlights, depending on the angle between the eye and the reflected light. Together, these components help produce more realistic lighting on 3D surfaces.

In the texture shader, we used UV coordinates passed from the vertex shader to sample a 2D texture. This allowed us to project an image onto the cloth surface. We confirmed it worked by replacing the texture with our own custom image and seeing it mapped correctly.

We used Texture_1 for both bump and displacement mapping. With bump mapping, the surface appeared detailed due to modified lighting normals, but the silhouette and actual geometry of the cloth and sphere remained unchanged. This made the bumps visible under lighting but flat in profile. Displacement mapping altered the actual vertex positions based on the height map. On the sphere, this caused the geometry to deform visibly, especially around edges and curved regions. When we used a coarse mesh (-o 16 -a 16), the bumps appeared jagged and blocky. Increasing the mesh resolution to -o 128 -a 128 produced much smoother displacement and more accurate surface detail. Therefore, bump mapping is more efficient and works well for subtle detail. Displacement mapping gives more realism and visible geometry changes, but requires a dense mesh to look good.
